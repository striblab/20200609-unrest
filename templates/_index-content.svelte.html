Following historic unrest, data show a quieter Minneapolis

By Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune June 15, 2020

While demonstrations continue nationwide following the deaths of George Floyd and other black people at police hands, Minneapolis call data show the city is quieter following an initial surge of unrest.

And even as the city continues reopening after months of pandemic-related shutdowns, police reports have fallen below average levels.

For instance, reports of gunshot-like noises from ShotSpotter and 911 calls spiked after Floyd's death before dipping to near silence. While ShotSpotter activations in Minneapolis detect many false positives, the microphone network seemingly captured several nights of turmoil.

 

Mapping police incidents, confirmed fires and gunfire reports day-by-day since Floyd's death captures that unrest, followed by activity quickly dissipating.

Minneapolis police and fire department activity swelled over those nights amid burning, looting and confrontations with protesters. Since the arrest of four officers for Floyd's murder, reported calls to those services are much lower than normal.

Police calls and stops, along with confirmed fires, are now down for June compared to last year. Meanwhile, calls to 311, the nonemergency line, dramatically increased as neighborhoods assessed property damage, streets were cleaned up and residents surveyed the initial aftermath.

It's unclear whether this decline in police activity is related to either residents making fewer calls to authorities, or to relatively quieter summer days during an ongoing pandemic where many events are still canceled and some businesses remain closed.

Mapping these incidents across the city from Floyd's death to three weekends later paints a stark picture of unrest concentrated primarily in Midtown and North Side Minneapolis, with most other places experiencing much lighter activity.
